Chapter 3: What is a Speaker?

A speaker is a transducer that converts electrical signals into sound waves. It consists of several key components, each playing a role in shaping the sound we hear.

1. Speaker Components

  • Drivers – The actual sound-producing parts of the speaker:
    • Tweeter (High Frequencies): Handles treble (above ~2 kHz).
    • Midrange Driver (Mid Frequencies): Covers vocals/instruments (~250 Hz – 2 kHz).
    • Woofer (Low Frequencies): Produces bass (~20 Hz – 500 Hz).
    • Subwoofer (Deep Bass): Handles sub-bass (below 80 Hz).
  • Crossover – An internal circuit that splits frequencies and sends them to the correct driver.
  • Cabinet (Enclosure) – The speaker box, affecting resonance and bass response.
  • Port (if present) – A hole or duct that enhances low-frequency output.

2. Speaker Properties

  • Impedance (Ω): The speaker’s electrical resistance. Common values: 4Ω, 6Ω, 8Ω. Lower impedance requires a more powerful amplifier.
  • Sensitivity (dB SPL @ 1W/1m): Measures how loud a speaker gets with 1 watt of power at 1 meter distance.
    Example: A 90 dB sensitivity speaker needs half the power of an 87 dB speaker for the same volume.
  • Frequency Response (Hz): The range of sound a speaker can reproduce. Example: 40 Hz – 20 kHz.
  • Power Handling (Watts RMS & Peak): How much power the speaker can handle continuously (RMS) and in short bursts (Peak).

3. Passive vs. Active Speakers

Passive Speakers: Require an external amplifier (most HiFi/home theater speakers).

Active Speakers: Have built-in amplifiers (like studio monitors or Bluetooth speakers).

4. Speaker Placement & Room Effects

Speaker Placement: Affects soundstage, imaging, and bass response.

Room Effects: Rooms can create reflections and standing waves, impacting clarity.

Solution: Correct placement and room treatment help improve sound.

5. Conclusion

This is the foundation of speakers—we’ll go deeper into crossover designs, frequency response graphs, and impedance curves in later chapters!
